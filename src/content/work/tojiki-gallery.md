Some projects are defined by the code, and some are defined by the people you build them with. Tojiki Gallery has firmly been the latter. The client came in with a clear sense of the world they wanted to build for their online store, and paired me with a designer whose job it was to shape that world visually. My role has been to take those designs and make them real inside Shopify, without losing the details that made them feel like Tojiki in the first place.

Rather than starting from a blank theme, we chose to customise an existing one as the foundation. It gave us a reliable baseline for cart, checkout, and the baseline ecommerce behaviours, and freed up the time we did have to focus on the parts of the site the customer actually notices. From there, most of the visible surface has been rebuilt, new components, new layouts, new interactions, stitched into the theme in a way that keeps it maintainable rather than turning it into something fragile.

A lot of the work has lived in the details. Typography that holds its rhythm across breakpoints, spacing that reads the same on a phone as it does on a desktop, transitions that feel intentional rather than decorative. Getting those things right inside Liquid and Shopify's section architecture takes a different kind of patience than building from scratch, you're working with the grain of the platform, not against it, and learning where it pushes back.

By the time launch came around, the project had settled into that final stretch where everything is about polish. Working this closely with a designer was a reminder that the best front-end work tends to happen when the handoff between design and development disappears.
